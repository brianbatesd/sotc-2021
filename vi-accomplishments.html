Những Thành Tựu trong năm 2021

Hội Đồng Thành Phố Vancouver đưa ra tầm nhìn cho cộng đồng và thông qua các chính sách và ngân sách để hỗ trợ cho tầm nhìn đó. Hơn 1.300 nhân viên của Thành Phố thực hiển triển khai tầm nhìn của họ và hỗ trợ cho một thành phố Vancouver an toàn, thân thiện, sôi động và thịnh vượng.

Một số thành tựu lớn trong năm vừa qua của Thành Phố bao gồm:

Đối phó với đại dịch COVID-19:

  • Operated the City-owned Tower Mall COVID testing site in partnership with Clark County Public Health in January 2021. The site became a drive-thru and walk-up COVID-19 vaccination site while continuing to offer free community testing from March to April 2021 – administering more than 11,000 tests and 40,000 vaccine doses.
  • Extended the Street Eats Program through Oct. 1, 2022, which, through special permitting and waived parking fees, helps restaurants add outdoor seating on sidewalks and streets. The program also provides an online map tool for customers to find businesses offering outdoor seating, services or curbside pickup.
  • Supported 90 businesses in industries most impacted by COVID-related restrictions through business license surcharge fee waivers totaling $76,500.
  • Vancouver’s Fire Department maintained full emergency fire and medical service staffing levels, despite significant daily exposures to COVID-19.
  • Awarded $386,783 in grants for public services and $477,040 for business assistance to respond to COVID-19 and aid in support or recovery of youth, families, individuals and local businesses.

Triển khai các hành động đa dạng, công bằng và hòa nhập:

  • Hired the City’s first Diversity, Equity and Inclusion Director.
  • Developed a three-year Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion strategic plan to help the City become more equitable and inclusive to the community it serves.
  • The City’s Diversity, Equity and Inclusion Director was appointed to serve on the Washington State LGBTQ Commission - the first person in Clark County to serve on this commission.
  • The City’s Parks, Recreation & Cultural Services department created and piloted a new park naming process, which provided the community with an opportunity to select a name for the new Nikkei Park. Nikkei Park honors the history of Japanese truck farmers who were removed and displaced from the land in the early 1900s.
  • The Community Task Force on Policing supported the City in addressing 74 of 84 recommendations included in a use-of-force study commissioned by the Vancouver Police Department to make policy and culture changes to improve equity, accountability, and transparency, promote higher standards, and increase public trust and confidence.
  • The Vancouver Police Department Chief’s Diversity Advisory Team (CDAT), a community-based volunteer advisory group, served on the Community Task Force on Policing and assisted in reviewing vendor proposals for a Police Camera Program.
  • Vancouver Police completed an initial review of vendors and a three-week pilot review of a body-worn and vehicle dash and in-car cameras system. Based on what was learned, the City is accepting additional vendor proposals and intends to have a comprehensive camera program in place in 2022.
  • Completed a six-month Driving While License Suspended Pre-Citation Diversion Pilot Program through the City Attorney’s Office. This program decriminalized most instances of Driving While License Suspended in the Third Degree, sometimes referred to as “driving while poor.” The systemic injustices that the City had begun repairing within our community ultimately became the topic of statewide discussion and were addressed through legal reforms at the Washington State Legislature and in the Courts.

Clean Community

Through working partnerships, led the way in cleaning up litter throughout Vancouver:

  • An estimated 1.28 tons of litter/debris was removed along highway ramps in the Interstate 5 corridor through a series of cleanup events. The City’s Solid Waste, Operations, Volunteer and Homeless Assistance and Resources Team programs are instrumental in these cleanups, supported by a $40,000 Community Litter Cleanup Program grant from the State Department of Ecology.
  • Talkin’ Trash, a Share program supported by a Vancouver Solid Waste grant, cleaned up nearly 291 tons of litter and debris across the city in 2021. In addition to improving community health and livability, the program offers Talkin’ Trash staff, who have experienced homelessness, work experience and training to assist in the transition toward stable housing.

Complete Streets

Continued to implement Complete Streets initiatives to support safer transportation no matter how you choose to get around, adding 2.2 miles of new protected bike lanes to Columbia Street in west Vancouver and completing the installation of mobility features for protected bike lanes on the Columbia Tech Center Drive Safety Improvement Project in east Vancouver.

New Park

Completed and opened a new park, Nikkei Park in east Vancouver. This five-acre neighborhood park features a unique playground, where visitors can interact with dinosaur-themed elements and dig for fossils.

Reimagined Events

Implemented new, small-scale and localized community events to keep neighbors safe and communities connected during the ongoing pandemic, including:

  • An eight-week summer series called Party in the Parks. This program took family-friendly activities into traditionally underserved neighborhoods two nights a week for eight weeks. The Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services Department provided music, games and art projects for families to enjoy.
  • First City-sponsored Día de Muertos celebration at Esther Short Park.

New Public Art

Supported the addition of five new public art pieces in downtown Vancouver, including The Columbia art collection by Larry Kirkland, Wind and Wave, and Art at the Marketplace.

A Vision for Essential Spaces

Adopted a new 10-year Vancouver Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services comprehensive plan developed with feedback from more than 3,800 community survey respondents and input from more than 1,600 residents at in-person engagement events.

Homelessness Response

  • The City hired a Homeless Response Coordinator who developed and began implementation of the City’s first Homelessness Response Plan to address the impacts of homelessness on community health, safety and cleanliness.
  • Vancouver City Council amended the City’s camping ordinance to facilitate creating supported Safe Stay Communities for unhoused residents. The amended laws prohibit unsanctioned camping within 1,000 feet of Safe Stay Communities and afford increased protections to the city’s most ecologically sensitive areas such as our water resources.
  • Opened the City’s first supported Safe Stay Community in east Vancouver, providing up to 40 residents with warm, dry and secure surroundings and access to high-quality, compassionate life-saving and social services while they work to transition out of homelessness. The community is staffed 24/7 by the non-profit service provider Outsiders Inn.
  • Hired an Encampment Response Coordinator who successfully managed the cleanup of more than 200 tons of solid waste from active and abandoned encampments.

Development Projects

  • Finalized a plan to transform central Vancouver’s Heights District – the new HX zone is intended to promote a flexible mix of residential, retail and employment land uses and a walkable land use pattern within The Heights District, allowing people to live and work within walking distance of each other. The former Tower Mall facility was demolished in preparation for future development.
  • A developer, Lincoln Property Company, was selected to master plan and develop the Waterfront Gateway – a 6.4-acre City-owned site connecting the downtown core and waterfront with a mix of office, commercial, retail and housing.
  • Successfully negotiated a development agreement for the Renaissance Boardwalk, a large mixed-use project on the Columbia Waterfront on the east side of the Interstate 5 Bridge, which will result in up to 220 apartment units on top of approximately 140,000 square feet of office, retail, and restaurant space.
  • In partnership with the Vancouver Housing Authority, broke ground on Fourth Plain Commons, a mixed-used development on Fourth Plain Boulevard that features affordable housing on the upper floors and a flexible community-serving space on the ground floor, including a co-working space, event space, commercial kitchen and outdoor public plaza.

Tourism Enhancements

Expanded tourism in Vancouver by increasing the City’s Lodging Tax contributions to Visit Vancouver Washington and established a 2022 lodging tax grant program to support local tourism events and projects following a three-year hiatus due to the pandemic. Visit Vancouver, the local tourism agency, is launching the city’s first tourism plan with the funds this year.

Transportation and Mobility Commission

Formed the City’s first Transportation and Mobility Commission, a diverse, 11-member volunteer group that provides advice to the city on a variety of transportation and mobility-related projects, programs and policies.

Housing and Business Grants

Awarded and expended nearly $2 million in grants for programs addressing homelessness, business assistance and affordable housing.

Vancouver Fire Department

  • Received $1.4 million in grant funding to support critical Fire Department operations, such as home fire prevention programming, officer development, special operations training, new thermal imaging cameras and emergency medical supplies needed in response to COVID-19.
  • Took delivery of one replacement and one new fire engine to help maintain current fire and emergency services demand and received three command staff vehicles to expand command officer response for larger incidents.
  • Started construction on new Fire Station 11 to improve emergency response in Vancouver and greater Clark County. The new station was funded in partnership with Fire District 5 and is anticipated to open October 2022.

Enhanced City Software

Upgraded the City’s Utility, Permitting, ePlans, and Work Order software systems to improve customer experience and City efficiency in processing permits requests, plan submittals, and work orders (e.g. service requests such as potholes or graffiti removal on public property).

Climate Change

City Council endorsed a target of an 80% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by the municipal government by 2025, and, for the entire city by 2030. The City also endorsed seeking a pathway to carbon neutrality for the entire Vancouver community by 2040. To jumpstart these efforts, an early action package of climate initiatives is now in progress.
